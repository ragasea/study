Electricity – Class 10 Notes

Electric circuit: A closed loop is required for current to flow and power devices. An open circuit breaks the loop, stopping current. A closed circuit completes the path so electrons move and lights/loads work. Electric current (I) is the rate of flow of electric charge (Q/t)11, usually via electrons in a conductor. Its SI unit is the ampere (A), equal to one coulomb per second12. Electric potential difference (voltage, V) is the work per unit charge to move charge between two points13. It “pushes” charges through the circuit; 1 V = 1 joule per coulomb.

Ohm’s Law: In ohmic materials, current is proportional to voltage14. We write V = I·R (hence I = V/R), where R is resistance (Ω). Resistance measures how much a material opposes current; materials with free electrons (metals) have low R, insulators (rubber, glass) have very high R. For a uniform wire of length ℓ and area A, R = ρℓ/A, where ρ is the resistivity of the material15. A longer or thinner wire has higher resistance.

Series and Parallel Circuits

In a series circuit, components are end-to-end in one path. The same current flows through each, and the voltages add. The equivalent resistance is the sum: Req=R₁+R₂+…16. In a parallel circuit, components share both connection points. Each branch has the same voltage, and the currents add. The reciprocal of the total resistance is the sum of reciprocals: 1/Req=1/R₁+1/R₂+…17. Parallel circuits have lower total R than any branch, allowing more current for a given voltage.

Electric Power

Electric power (P) is the rate of energy transfer, given by P = V·I18 (voltage times current). In other forms, P = I²R or P = V²/R. Power is in watts (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s. For example, if 2 A flows through a 5 Ω resistor at 10 V, P = 10×2 = 20 W of power (dissipated as heat). Household appliances (lamps, heaters) are rated in watts, reflecting this heating effect of current.

Practice Questions

Q1: Why does a bulb glow only when the circuit is closed?
Answer: A closed circuit completes the path for current. In a closed loop, current flows through the bulb so it lights. In an open circuit the path is broken, so no current reaches the bulb and it stays off.
Q2: State Ohm’s law with its formula.
Answer: Ohm’s law states that the current (I) through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage (V) across it (at constant temperature)19. Mathematically: V = I·R, where R is the resistance.
Q3: Calculate the equivalent resistance of a 10 Ω and a 30 Ω resistor in parallel.
Answer: Use 1/Req = 1/10 + 1/30 = (3+1)/30 = 4/30. So Req = 30/4 = 7.5 Ω.
Q4: A wire has ρ=1.6×10–8 Ω·m, length 2 m, area 1 mm². Find its resistance.
Answer: R = ρℓ/A = (1.6×10–8 Ω·m)(2 m) / (1×10–6 m²) = 3.2×10–2 Ω.

Quick Formula Summary

  • Current: I = Q/t (ampere, A = C/s)
  • Voltage: V = W/Q (volt, V = J/C)
  • Ohm’s Law: V = I·R
  • Resistance: R = ρℓ/A
  • Series R: Req = R₁ + R₂ + …
  • Parallel R: 1/Req = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + …
  • Power: P = V·I = I²R = V²/R (watt)
