Carbon: The Element of Life

Atomic Data

  • Abundance: 15th most in Earth's crust.
  • Atomic Number: 6
  • Atomic Mass: 12u
  • Valency: 4 (Tetravalency)
  • Electrons (K, L Shells): 2, 4

Covalent Bonding

Chemical bond involving the sharing of electrons to form electron pairs between atoms.

Types: Single, Double, and Triple Covalent Bonds.

Covalent Compound Properties

  • Low melting/boiling points (weak intermolecular forces).
  • Poor conductors (lack charged particles).
  • Generally soluble in organic solvents, insoluble in water (except sugar).

Allotropes: The Physical Forms

üíé Diamond

3D network, each carbon bonds with four others; very hard.

  • Highly stable (four strong covalent bonds).
  • Uses: Jewelry, abrasives.

‚úèÔ∏è Graphite

Layers of hexagons held by weak forces; soft and slippery.

  • Three covalent bonds + delocalized electrons (conducts electricity).
  • Uses: Pencils, lubricants.

‚öΩ Fullerene (C60)

Hollow, cage-like structure (soccer ball shape).

  • Strong covalent bonds in hexagons and pentagons.
  • Uses: Nanotech, drugs (unique electronic properties).

The Four Powers of Carbon

  • **Catenation:** Forms strong covalent bonds with itself (chains, branches, rings).
  • **Tetravalency:** Forms four covalent bonds for stability and diverse compounds.
  • **Isomerism:** Compounds with the same molecular formula but different structures.
  • **Polymerisation:** Small molecules (monomers) join to form large molecules (polymers).

Homologous Series

  • Same general formula.
  • Differ by a -CH2 group.
  • Molecular mass difference is 14 Œº.
  • Similar chemical properties, gradual change in physical properties.

Key Chemical Reactions

üî• Combustion (Burning)

Carbon compounds burn in O2 to form CO2, H2O, and heat.

CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O + Heat

üß™ Oxidation

Alcohols are oxidized to acids using oxidizing agents like KMnO4 or K2Cr2O7.

CH3CH2OH + [O] → CH3COOH

‚ûï Addition Reaction (Hydrogenation)

Unsaturated hydrocarbons (alkenes, alkynes) add H2 in the presence of Ni/Pd catalyst (converting oils to solid fats).

CH2=CH2 + H2 → CH3-CH3

üîÑ Substitution Reaction

Alkanes react with halogens (Cl2, Br2) in the presence of sunlight.

CH4 + Cl2  (→ Sunlight) CH3Cl + HCl

The Star Compounds

Ethanol (C2H5OH)

  • Active ingredient in alcoholic drinks; liquid at room temperature; good solvent.
  • **Denatured Alcohol:** Ethanol mixed with poisonous chemicals (like methanol) to make it unfit for drinking (used in industry).
  • **Rx with Sodium:** 2Na + 2C2H5OH → 2C2H5O-Na+ + H2 (Produces Hydrogen gas)
  • **Dehydration:** Heated with hot conc. H2SO4 at 443 K (Dehydrating Agent) to form Ethene (C2H5OH → CH2=CH2 + H2O).

Ethanoic Acid (CH3COOH)

  • Commonly Acetic Acid; 5-8% solution in water is **Vinegar** (preservative).
  • **Glacial Acetic Acid:** Freezes in winter (Melting point: 290 K).
  • **Esterification:** Reacts with Ethanol (conc. H2SO4) to form sweet-smelling **Ester** (used in perfumes).
  • **Rx with Bases:** CH3COOH + NaOH → CH3COONa + H2O
  • **Rx with Carbonates/Hydrogencarbonates:** Produces CO2, water, and Sodium Acetate.

üßº Soaps, Detergents & Water

Micelle Formation (The Cleansing Process)

  • Soap molecule has a **Hydrophilic Head** (Water-attracting, polar) and a **Hydrophobic Tail** (Oil/Dirt-attracting, non-polar).
  • The hydrophobic tails attach to the dirt/grease.
  • Molecules arrange themselves in a spherical **Micelle** structure, trapping the dirt inside.
  • When rinsed, the water-attracting heads carry the micelles (with the trapped dirt) away.

Hard Water vs. Soft Water

  • **Hard Water:** High mineral content (Ca2+, Mg2+). Forms **scum** and **scale** and does not lather easily with soap.
  • **Soft Water:** Low mineral content (Na+, K+). Lathers easily; feels smooth on skin and hair.

Soap vs. Detergent

Composition Sodium/potassium salts of long-chain carboxylic acids. Sodium salts of sulphonic acids or ammonium salts with chloride/bromide.
Hard Water Not very effective (forms scum). Effective even in hard water.
Cleansing Action Relatively weak. Strong.
Biodegradability Biodegradable. Most are non-biodegradable.
